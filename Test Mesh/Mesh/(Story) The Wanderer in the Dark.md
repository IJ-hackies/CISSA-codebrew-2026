# The Wanderer in the Dark

## Introduction

The last hand Javier Moreno ever played was a king-high straight against a full house. He had read the table perfectly -- the micro-expressions, the betting patterns, the pause before the river card that told him his opponent was bluffing. He was right about all of it. He was right about everything except the cards. The flop had given him the world, and the river took it away, and the $1.4 million that crossed the felt that night carried with it the last thing Jav had ever trusted, which was his own judgment.

He walked away from the circuit after that. Not dramatically -- no scene, no speech, no last drink at the casino bar. He simply did not show up to the next tournament. Or the one after that. He moved into a one-bedroom apartment in Henderson, Nevada, ate frozen burritos, and spent eighteen months watching nature documentaries with the sound off because the narration annoyed him. The animals, at least, did not pretend to know what they were doing. They simply acted and survived or didn't.

It was during this period of distinguished unemployment that the recruitment notice found him. A mission to unmapped galaxies. No prior data. No established protocols. No models of what the planets would look like or how they would behave. Just an agent -- human, in this case -- dropped into an unknown environment and expected to figure things out through trial and error. The notice described the feedback mechanism: each planet would provide signals. Positive for good decisions, negative for bad ones. No explanations, no appeals, no second opinions. Just the raw score.

Jav read the notice twice. Then he laughed for the first time in over a year.

He understood [[Workspace/Repos/Main/Test Mesh/Mesh/(Concept) Uncertainty]] the way a fish understands water -- immersed in it so completely that he had forgotten it was there. His entire career had been an exercise in making decisions with incomplete information, in acting before the full picture arrived because the full picture never arrived. But understanding that you live in uncertainty and understanding uncertainty itself are two different things. He had always played the odds. He had never stopped to ask what the odds were made of.

The tension of [[Workspace/Repos/Main/Test Mesh/Mesh/(Concept) Exploration versus Exploitation]] had defined every session he had ever played. Do you stick with the strategy that has been winning -- the tight-aggressive play, the predictable folds, the reliable grind? Or do you deviate, try something reckless, bluff into a hand you have no business contesting? Exploitation was comfortable. Exploitation paid the bills. But every truly great player Jav had known was someone who could sense when the table had shifted, when the old pattern was dead, and who had the nerve to abandon what was working in search of what might work better. Jav had been an exploiter. He wondered now if that was why the river card had surprised him.

And then there was [[Workspace/Repos/Main/Test Mesh/Mesh/(Concept) The Reward Signal]] -- the only language he had ever fully trusted. Not words, not intentions, not the stories people told about why they did what they did. Just the outcome. Did the bet pay off or didn't it? Did the play work or didn't it? The chip stack at the end of the night was the only honest thing in the room. It could not be argued with. It could not be charmed. It simply was. The mission promised nothing else: no manuals, no mentors, no explanations. Just a signal that said yes or no, more or less, better or worse. For a man who had lost faith in every other form of feedback, this felt less like a job and more like a homecoming.

He signed the papers on a Tuesday. Packed a bag that weighed eleven pounds. Told no one, because there was no one left to tell.

The ship was small and functional, designed for a crew of one. Jav did not mind. He had spent thirty years reading rooms full of people and had concluded that the average human being was a noisy signal with poor calibration. Solitude, at least, had a decent signal-to-noise ratio.

He pulled up the navigation chart. Ten planets, strung across two uncharted galaxies like lights on a wire. No descriptions. No warnings. Just coordinates and names.

He pointed the ship toward the first one and did what he had always done: he played the hand he was dealt.

planet: [[Workspace/Repos/Main/Test Mesh/Mesh/(Planet) The Atari Agent]]

The first planet looked like a fever dream in a neon arcade. Jav descended through an atmosphere that flickered -- literally flickered, as though the sky were a CRT monitor running at a refresh rate just slow enough to notice. The surface resolved into vast grids of coloured pixels, shifting and rearranging in patterns that reminded him of nothing so much as the old Atari games he had played as a kid in his uncle's garage in Tucson. Pac-Man ghosts the size of buildings drifted through geometric mazes. A Breakout paddle the length of a football field swept back and forth across a canyon, deflecting a boulder that arced upward and shattered crystalline bricks from the ceiling.

He landed on a flat expanse of green pixels and stepped out. The air tasted like static electricity.

Immediately, he noticed something: nothing here came with instructions. The sprites moved according to rules he could not see. The paddle responded to something -- maybe his presence, maybe a signal he could not detect -- but no one told him what it was doing or why. A number in the corner of the sky changed every few seconds. Sometimes it went up. Sometimes it went down. That was it. That was all the feedback the planet offered.

Jav stood there and felt something he had not felt in a long time: the specific, almost physical sensation of not knowing the rules but being expected to play anyway. This was not a game you studied before sitting down. This was a game you learned by losing.

He watched the Breakout paddle for an hour, tracking the relationship between its movements and the score in the sky. Slowly, painfully, he began to see it. The paddle was not following a script. It was an agent -- observing pixels, taking actions, receiving rewards. It had learned to play from nothing but raw feedback. No one had explained what bricks were. No one had defined the physics. The agent had sat down at the cabinet cold, pushed the joystick, watched what happened, and done it again. Thousands of times. Millions, maybe.

It was, Jav realised, the purest form of the game he had ever seen. No bluffing. No psychology. Just observation, action, reward. The primal hand.

He got back on the ship and stared at the ceiling for a while. Then he pulled up the next coordinates.

planet: [[Workspace/Repos/Main/Test Mesh/Mesh/(Planet) The Rat Experiment]]

The second planet was small and warm and smelled like wet fur.

Jav found it in a clearing: a single lever, a single light, and a bell mounted on a post. The apparatus looked like something from a psychology lab, scaled up to the size of a shed. No instructions. No signage. He walked around it twice, hands in his pockets, reading it the way he used to read a new table -- looking for the tell, the asymmetry, the thing that did not belong.

He pulled the lever. The light went off. He pulled it again. The bell rang. Then a jolt of static electricity ran up his arm -- not painful, exactly, but deeply unpleasant. He jerked his hand back and swore.

Second attempt. Bell rang first this time. Light went off. He pulled the lever twice. A compartment opened and dispensed something that looked and tasted like extremely good cheese.

He sat down on the ground and thought about what had just happened. Same lever. Same bell. Same light. Different sequence, different outcome. Shock or cheese. The planet was running an experiment on him, and the variable was not what he did but what he remembered about the order in which things had happened.

This was a question about state -- about what information you carry forward and what you discard. If he tracked only the last few events, the lever-light-lever-bell sequence matched the first episode: expect a shock. If he tracked only the counts -- how many times each event had occurred -- it matched the second: expect cheese. If he tried to remember everything, neither episode matched, and he had nothing.

Jav had faced this exact problem at the poker table. What do you remember? The last three hands? The overall pattern of the session? Every card ever dealt? Too little memory and you cannot tell a bluff from a value bet. Too much and you drown in noise. The right memory wraps up exactly enough of the past to predict the future. No more, no less.

He pulled the lever one more time, watching carefully. He was beginning to understand that on these planets, the first question was never what to do. It was what to notice.

planet: [[Workspace/Repos/Main/Test Mesh/Mesh/(Planet) The Reward Hypothesis]]

The third planet announced itself before he landed. A single sentence, carved into a stone tablet the size of a billboard at the edge of the landing zone: ALL GOALS CAN BE DESCRIBED BY THE MAXIMISATION OF EXPECTED CUMULATIVE REWARD.

Jav read it and laughed. "That's a hell of a claim," he said to no one.

But the planet seemed determined to prove it. He found a series of environments arranged like exhibition halls in a museum. In the first, a humanoid figure walked a tightrope over a chasm, receiving visible pulses of light each time it took a step forward and a visible shadow each time it wobbled. Negative for instability, positive for progress. The figure's entire goal -- this impossibly complex act of balance, coordination, muscle control -- had been compressed into a single number. Walk forward: good. Fall: bad. Everything else emerged from that.

In the second hall, a simulated warehouse cycled through seasons, stocking and depleting shelves. Costs for holding, penalties for empty shelves, revenue for sales. All of it collapsed into a single scalar signal. In the third, a chess game where the only feedback was win, lose, or draw -- three numbers encoding the entire richness of strategy.

The audacity of it struck him. Every goal, no matter how complex, no matter how multi-dimensional, reduced to a single number accumulated over time. The reward hypothesis was not just a claim about how agents should learn. It was a claim about the nature of purpose itself. That everything you could want could be expressed as more or less of one thing.

Jav thought about his own life. Had his goals ever been anything other than a single number? The chip count. The bankroll. The ranking. He had spent thirty years reducing every decision to its impact on a single scalar signal, and he had never noticed that this was a philosophy, not just a habit.

The stone tablet cast a long shadow in the alien light. Jav walked through it and kept going. The hypothesis was planted, and he could feel it taking root.

planet: [[Workspace/Repos/Main/Test Mesh/Mesh/(Planet) Value Functions]]

The fourth planet was a grid.

Not metaphorically -- the surface was literally divided into cells, each one roughly the size of a room, each one glowing with a number. Some cells showed -1. Some showed -7. One, at the far edge of the visible expanse, showed 0. The numbers created a gradient, a landscape of goodness and badness that he could read at a glance.

Jav stepped into a cell marked -12 and felt a faint, persistent discomfort -- not pain, but a low hum of wrongness, like the feeling of holding a losing hand. He moved to the adjacent cell, marked -11, and the hum diminished slightly. He moved again. -10. The wrongness receded further.

He was following the gradient downhill. Not because anyone told him to, but because the numbers created an irresistible pull. Each cell's value was not the immediate reward for standing in it -- it was the total expected future reward from that point forward. A cell marked -12 was twelve units of cumulative suffering away from the goal. A cell marked -1 was practically there. The value function had transformed the entire grid into a map of consequences, and the optimal path was simply the path that followed the numbers toward zero.

This was, he realised, what he had always tried to do at the poker table. Not just evaluate the current hand -- evaluate the current position. How much future value did this seat, this stack, this table composition represent? A strong hand in a weak position was worth less than a mediocre hand in a strong one, because value was not about now. It was about the cascade of decisions that flowed from now into every possible future.

The cell marked 0 was the goal. He stood in it and felt nothing -- no hum, no discomfort, no elation. Just neutral. Zero. The absence of future cost. It was the most peaceful thing he had experienced in years.

He understood now: you had to solve the prediction problem before you could solve the control problem. You had to know what things were worth before you could decide what to do. Evaluation before action. Always.

planet: [[Workspace/Repos/Main/Test Mesh/Mesh/(Planet) Computing Returns]]

The fifth planet ran on a clock.

Every surface pulsed with a slow, steady rhythm, and Jav quickly learned that the rhythm was a discount factor -- a parameter that determined how much the future mattered relative to the present. The planet seemed to exist in multiple temporal modes simultaneously, and he could toggle between them by adjusting a dial on a pedestal at the centre of the main clearing.

He set the dial to zero and watched the world go flat. Immediate rewards blazed with intensity -- a fruit tree nearby practically glowed -- but anything more than a few steps away faded to grey. With the discount at zero, only the next moment existed. The myopic view. A bar offering +1 right now outshone a university offering +10 in four years. The future was invisible.

He turned the dial to 0.9 and the world transformed. Distant rewards swelled into view like stars emerging at dusk. The fruit tree dimmed to a pleasant background glow, while far-off structures -- representing long-term investments, deferred payoffs, patient strategies -- blazed with accumulated importance. The studying costs were front-loaded and full-weight, but the distant +10 for graduating loomed so large it justified every intermediate -2 along the way.

He turned the dial to 1.0 and the planet screamed. Every reward, no matter how distant, carried full weight. An infinite loop of small penalties -- a self-reinforcing cycle of scrolling through social media, say -- accumulated to infinite negative value. The planet could not stabilise. Discounting, he understood now, was not just a preference about the future. It was a mathematical necessity. Without it, infinite horizons produced infinite values, and infinite values made comparison impossible.

Jav turned the dial back to 0.9 and let the world settle. He thought about all the times he had played the short game at the table -- taking a small pot now instead of setting up a larger one later. A discount factor of 0.3, maybe. He had been calibrated wrong for years. The same environment, the same actions, the same rewards -- but a single parameter reshaped what counted as optimal. His relationship to time had never been fixed by the world. It had always been a choice.

planet: [[Workspace/Repos/Main/Test Mesh/Mesh/(Planet) Expected Utility Maximisation]]

The sixth planet was a desert with two paths.

One path led left, toward a structure that looked like shelter. The other led right, into open dunes where something glinted in the distance. A weather system churned overhead -- maybe rain, maybe just cloud. Jav could not tell.

At the fork, a stone slab displayed a formula. For each action, compute the sum of the probability of each outcome multiplied by the utility of that outcome. Choose the action with the highest sum. Below it, an example: the umbrella problem. Bring the umbrella or leave it. Probability of sun. Probability of rain. Utility of each combination. Multiply, sum, compare, decide.

Jav stared at the formula and felt something click into place. This was what he had been doing his entire career, but without the notation. Every bet he had ever placed was an expected value calculation -- the probability that his hand was best, multiplied by the pot he would win, minus the probability that he was beaten, multiplied by the amount he would lose. He had never written it down, but it had lived in his fingers, in the speed at which he counted outs and estimated odds.

The power of the formula, he saw now, was that it combined two things that felt fundamentally different -- what you believe about the world and what you want from the world -- into a single number that supported comparison. Beliefs and preferences, probability and utility, fused into a decision. You did not need to resolve your uncertainty before acting. You could act optimally within your uncertainty. That was the trick. That was the whole trick.

He took the left path. The shelter was real. The rain came twenty minutes later.

Standing dry under the roof, listening to the downpour, he thought about all the times he had waited for certainty before making a move. All the hands he had folded because the situation was unclear. Expected utility did not require clarity. It required only honest probabilities and honest preferences. The decision was not "what will happen" but "what should I do given what might happen." He had been asking the wrong question for thirty years.

planet: [[Workspace/Repos/Main/Test Mesh/Mesh/(Planet) The Exploration-Exploitation Examples]]

The seventh planet was a city.

Four districts, four dilemmas. In the first, a row of restaurants lined a boulevard. Jav recognised the setup immediately: exploitation was the place on the corner that he knew was decent. Exploration was the unmarked door two blocks down that might be extraordinary or might give him food poisoning. He had eaten at the same three restaurants in Henderson for eighteen months. He knew which one he was.

In the second district, digital billboards cycled through advertisements. The system was tracking click-through rates, showing the highest-performing ad by default but occasionally swapping in something untested. Exploitation was the proven ad. Exploration was the wild card. The system was not just serving content -- it was learning, using each impression as data to update its model of what worked.

The third district was a mining operation. Drills at known sites versus drills at untested coordinates. The satellite data was good but not perfect. The best-known location might not be the best actual location. The only way to find out was to spend resources on uncertainty.

The fourth district was a game parlour. Players at tables, some grinding out reliable strategies, others trying bizarre experimental moves that failed spectacularly -- or occasionally uncovered something no one had seen before.

A thought struck him, quiet and precise: children are better at this. He had watched his niece play with a toy kitchen once, combining objects in ways that made no functional sense -- putting the plastic egg in the shoe, stirring the crayon in the pot. She was not being foolish. She was exploring. Her willingness to try nonsensical actions was precisely what let her discover the structure of her environment so rapidly. Adults exploit more and explore less. Efficient in known domains. Slow to learn in new ones.

Jav stood in the middle of the city and admitted something he had been circling for ten planets' worth of denial: he had stopped exploring. After the bad beat, he had retreated into the known -- the apartment, the frozen burritos, the nature documentaries. Pure exploitation of a miserable equilibrium. He had been so afraid of another bad outcome that he had locked himself into the worst one.

Epsilon-greedy, the planet whispered. With probability epsilon, take a random action. With probability one minus epsilon, take the best known one. Tuning epsilon is tuning curiosity.

He needed to turn his epsilon back up.

planet: [[Workspace/Repos/Main/Test Mesh/Mesh/(Planet) The Humanoid Robot]]

The eighth planet was a workshop.

In the centre of it, a humanoid figure was learning to walk. Jav watched from a raised platform as the thing fell, got up, fell again, got up again, fell in a new direction, stumbled three steps, fell, stumbled four steps, wobbled, caught itself, fell. The cycle was mesmerizing in its persistence. The figure had received only two signals: negative reward for falling over, positive reward for forward motion. That was all. No instructions about joint angles, balance points, or gait patterns. Just the penalty and the incentive.

The early episodes were catastrophic. The figure toppled immediately and repeatedly, accumulating negative reward in heaps. But Jav noticed something in the pattern of failures. Each fall was slightly different. The figure was not repeating the same mistake -- it was exploring the space of possible mistakes, and some mistakes were less costly than others. Certain joint configurations produced brief forward motion before collapse. The figure reinforced those configurations, extended them, assembled fragments of movement into something that, after what must have been thousands of iterations, began to resemble a gait.

He leaned forward on the railing and watched the thing take its first uninterrupted sequence of ten steps. It was ugly -- lurching, asymmetric, nothing like human locomotion. But it was walking. It had taught itself to walk using nothing but a number.

The fragility of the design fascinated him. How large should the penalty for falling be? Too large and the figure became paralysed with caution -- it would rather stand still and receive zero than risk moving and receiving negative. Too small and it took reckless risks, crashing constantly. Should forward motion be rewarded per step or cumulatively? Per-step rewards encouraged speed. Cumulative rewards encouraged distance. The "simple" reward signal concealed a design space as complex as the behaviour it produced.

Jav thought about his own reward signals -- the ones he had designed for himself without knowing he was designing them. The penalty for losing had been set too high. After the bad beat, the magnitude of the fall penalty had overwhelmed everything, and he had frozen. Too cautious to move. Too scared of negative reward to accept any risk at all. He had become the paralysed robot, standing still in the middle of the workshop floor, optimising for the absence of pain rather than the presence of progress.

He needed to recalibrate.

planet: [[Workspace/Repos/Main/Test Mesh/Mesh/(Planet) The Warehouse Inventory Problem]]

The ninth planet was an economy.

Jav found himself in a warehouse district stretching to the horizon, shelves stacked with goods that appeared and disappeared according to rhythms he could not immediately parse. Demand surged and ebbed. Seasons changed. Holiday rushes stripped shelves bare, and quiet periods left inventory accumulating dust and cost.

The central question was deceptively simple: restock now and pay the holding cost, or wait and risk a stockout during the rush? Every action's consequences unfolded not immediately but over days and weeks. Restocking cost money now but prevented lost sales later. Not restocking saved money now but created risk. The future was uncertain, the feedback was delayed, and the horizon was long.

This was not an arcade game. There were no discrete episodes, no clear terminal states, no moment when the screen flashed GAME OVER and the score was tallied. This was continuous, noisy, economic life. The states encoded inventory levels and seasons. The actions were restock quantities. The transitions reflected uncertain demand -- how many units would customers buy tomorrow? The rewards were negative for holding costs and lost sales, positive for successful fulfilment. The discount factor captured the time value of money: capital spent on inventory today could have been invested elsewhere.

Jav recognised this problem. Not from a textbook -- from the felt. Tournament poker was a warehouse problem. Your chip stack was your inventory. Blinds were holding costs, eating away at your position every round. Going all-in was a massive restock -- high immediate cost, high potential reward, catastrophic downside. The optimal strategy was not a fixed rule but a function of stack size, blind level, table position, and opponent tendencies. It was sequential, uncertain, delayed, and exactly the kind of problem where the ability to handle uncertainty and long-term dependencies provided genuine practical value.

The reward hypothesis had followed him from the third planet to the ninth, and it proved its worth here in a domain far removed from pixel grids and lever-pulling rats. Every goal -- even the sprawling, multi-variable, seasonally adjusted goal of running a profitable warehouse -- could be expressed as a number. The number was complex and carefully designed, but it was still, at bottom, a single scalar signal. And the agent's job was still, at bottom, to make that number as large as possible over time.

planet: [[Workspace/Repos/Main/Test Mesh/Mesh/(Planet) Model-Free versus Model-Based Learning]]

The tenth and final planet was split in two.

One hemisphere was transparent. Jav could see the internal machinery -- the gears, the probability distributions, the transition functions laid bare like the anatomy of a clock. An agent on this side could simulate any action before taking it, could plan entire trajectories without moving, could evaluate policies in its head. This was the model-based hemisphere: the agent that knew the rules, or had learned them, and could reason forward from any state.

The other hemisphere was opaque. The agent on this side saw only what it experienced -- the stream of states, actions, and rewards that constituted its actual life. No simulation. No internal model. No planning without acting. It learned the value of things by encountering them, one episode at a time, updating its estimates from lived experience. This was the model-free hemisphere: the agent that did not know the rules and did not try to learn them. It learned the answers directly.

Jav walked the border between the two hemispheres and understood the trade-off with the immediacy of personal experience. The model-based agent was sample-efficient -- every interaction provided data about the model, and the model could be queried endlessly for planning. But it was fragile. If the model was wrong, the agent planned optimally in a fictional world. It could be brilliantly, rigorously, catastrophically mistaken. The model-free agent was robust -- it learned from real outcomes, not simulated ones. But it was hungry. Every evaluation required actual interaction, and it could not think ahead without acting.

He had been both of these agents. Early in his career, he had been model-free -- sitting at low-stakes tables, learning the value of hands and positions through direct experience, one session at a time. Slow, expensive, but grounded in reality. Later, he had become model-based -- building mental models of opponents, simulating future hands, planning three streets ahead. Faster, more efficient, but vulnerable to the moment when the model broke. The bad beat had not been a failure of luck. It had been a failure of his model. He had been planning optimally in a fictional world.

The frontier, the planet seemed to whisper, was hybrid. Learn a model where you can. Use it for planning. Fall back to model-free updates where the model is unreliable. Do not commit to one hemisphere. Walk the border.

Jav took one last look at both sides, the transparent and the opaque, and headed back to the ship.

## Conclusion

Ten planets. Ten lessons dealt face-up.

Jav sat in the pilot's seat and stared at the navigation chart, now populated with notes and annotations in his own cramped handwriting. The coordinates were the same, but the map had changed because he had changed, and he understood now that the map was never separate from the mapmaker.

The first pattern that had crystallised, somewhere between the rat and the warehouse, was what he came to think of as [[Workspace/Repos/Main/Test Mesh/Mesh/(Concept) Agent Perspectives]]. The same problem looked completely different depending on what the agent knew. A model-based agent and a model-free agent could inhabit the same world and extract entirely different lessons from the same experience. The agent with the model used each transition to update its understanding of how the world worked. The agent without a model used the same transition to update its estimate of how much reward to expect. Same data. Different questions. Different answers. Neither perspective was wrong -- each traded one vulnerability for another. He had spent his career locked into one perspective at a time, never realising that the choice of perspective was itself a decision, and perhaps the most consequential one.

The second pattern was [[Workspace/Repos/Main/Test Mesh/Mesh/(Concept) State Design for MDPs]] -- the question that had haunted him since the rat planet. What counts as a state? What do you carry forward and what do you leave behind? The rat's prediction depended entirely on how it encoded its history. The grid world's dynamics depended on whether velocity was in the state. The warehouse's optimal policy depended on whether season and demand forecasts were included. Too little information and the agent could not distinguish situations requiring different actions. Too much and the space of possibilities exploded into unmanageable noise. The right representation wrapped up exactly enough of the past to predict the future. No more, no less.

He thought about his own state design. After the bad beat, he had been carrying too much. Every hand he had ever lost, every tell he had ever misread, every river card that had betrayed him -- all of it packed into his representation of the present moment, making every new decision feel like a referendum on every old failure. His state space had exploded, and he had become intractable. The lesson of the rat was that memory is not a virtue in itself. The right memory is a virtue. The rest is weight.

Ten planets, and what had they taught him? That uncertainty is not a deficiency but a condition. That exploration is not a cost but an investment. That the reward signal is the only honest language and that designing it well is the deepest problem of all. That value is not about the present but about the expected future. That your relationship to time is a choice, not a fact. That the decision rule accommodates uncertainty rather than waiting for it to resolve. That curiosity is a parameter you can tune. That learning from failure is not a platitude but a mechanism. That the world does not owe you a model, and that sometimes the most robust path is the one learned step by painful step from direct experience.

He had gone out as a man who had lost faith in his own judgment. He returned as something different -- not confident, exactly, but calibrated. He knew now that the bad beat had not been a flaw in his thinking. It had been the expected cost of operating under uncertainty, a cost that every agent in every world must pay. The river card was not a betrayal. It was a sample from a distribution. He had been unlucky, not wrong. And the eighteen months of frozen burritos and silent documentaries had been the consequence of a misdesigned reward signal -- a fall penalty set so high that paralysis became optimal.

He adjusted his epsilon. Not all the way up -- he was not a child pressing random buttons. But enough. Enough to try a restaurant he had never been to. Enough to play a hand he was not sure about. Enough to trust that the information gained from a loss could be worth more than the comfort of a fold.

The ship turned toward home. Jav watched the stars rearrange themselves into unfamiliar constellations and thought, for the first time in a long time, that he might sit down at a table again. Not because he was certain he would win. But because he finally understood that certainty was never the point.

The point was the signal. The point was always the signal.

And the signal said: play.
